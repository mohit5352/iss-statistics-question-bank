58. [Topic: Generalized Linear Model]
For uncorrelated observations \( (y_1, y_2, \dots, y_n) = \mathbf{Y} \), we define a linear model-I. \( \mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\beta + \epsilon \) such that \( E(\epsilon) = 0 \), \( D(\epsilon) = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I} \), \( E(\mathbf{Y}) = \mathbf{X}\beta \) and \( D(\mathbf{Y}) = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I} \). We then define a more general model-II. \( E(\mathbf{Y}) = \mathbf{X}\beta \) and \( D(\mathbf{Y}) = \sigma^2 \mathbf{G} \), where \( |\mathbf{G}| \ne 0 \) introduces correlations among the observations such that \( \mathbf{G} \) is a known matrix, \( \mathbf{I} \) is a unit matrix of order n, D is a variance-covariance matrix and \( \beta, \sigma^2 \) are unknown. Model-II can be reduced to Model-I by considering a transformation.
(a) \( \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{G}^{1/2}\mathbf{Y}\mathbf{G}^{-1/2} \)
(b) \( \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{G}^{-1/2}\mathbf{Y} \)
(c) \( \mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{Y}\mathbf{G}^{-1/2} \)
(d) None of the above
